To those who make things,

You didn't take up engineering to name a face. You took it up because you like it when a thing fits — when the tolerance stacks, when the bracket lands on the bolt circle, when the part comes off the machine right.

Somewhere between the idea and the drawing, the work turned into operation. Pick the plane. Set the constraint. Rename the reference. Rebuild, and watch it fail three features deep because a chamfer moved.

So describe the part instead — the way you'd say it to someone across the bench. A plate, 100 × 50 × 10, two ⌀15 holes on a 50 pitch. What comes back is a model with its features intact: sketches you can open, dimensions you can drive, a history you can edit.

Not a mesh. Not a picture of a part. A solid that rebuilds to the same cubic millimetre, every time.

The geometry is still yours. The bookkeeping isn't your job anymore. This is for you.

From a sentence to an editable model.

Three movements, in order. Every model is a feature history you can open, change, and rebuild — the same way you'd work in CAD.

02 What it guarantees

Engineering-grade, not a toy renderer.

The properties that decide whether a generated model is worth anything on a Monday morning.

Feature history
Sketches, pads, pockets and patterns you can reopen and re-dimension. Geometry stays driven by parameters, not baked into triangles.
Sketch constraints
Profiles carry their dimensions and relations, so an edit moves what it should and holds the rest.
Deterministic rebuild
A model rebuilds to the same solid every time — verified against volume and topology, not eyeballed.
Stable references
Faces and edges are pinned by geometric signature, so an edit survives instead of breaking on a renamed face.
Exports
STEP · STL · GLB. Into your CAD, your slicer, or the browser. Standard formats, no lock-in.
No black box
Every model compiles to industry-standard STEP you can open anywhere. Nothing is trapped in a proprietary format.
